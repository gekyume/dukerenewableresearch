PhotoVoltaics

PHOTO VOLTAICS

Summary

The solar cell market of today relies heavily on silicon technology – around 80% of all cells produced are silicon based. The process of acquiring high quality materials to develop relatively thick cells is expensive, and unsustainable.

Enter Dr. Mitzi. His research lab is dedicated to finding alternate materials to use for the development of solar cells. Specifically, the group is researching two types of materials: perovskites and chalcogenides. Perovskites, a combination of organic and inorganic materials, allow for high absorption of the sun’s energy across the electromagnetic spectrum. Chalcogenides, compounds made from tellurium, selenium, or sulfur, can be used to develop thin film solar cells. Together, these materials provide a cost effective alternative to silicon, allowing solar power to become more commercially available.

Publications
  • Device Performance of Emerging Photovoltaic Materials
    • Osbel Almora, Derya Baran, David B. Mitzi
    • Following the initial report in 2020, this paper details the efficacy of several emerging photovoltaic (e-PV) cell materials, including organic , dye sensitized, and perovskite solar cells. The report details the best research solar cells, based on categories such as flexibility and efficiency.
  • Impact of Structural Distortions on the Optoelectronic and Spin-Related Properties of Two-Dimensional Hybrid Perovskites
    • Manoj K. Jana, Yi Xie and David B. Mitzi
    • This report discusses the structural nature of metal halide perovskites (a promising alternative material to silicon for PV cells). Moreover, the paper outlines various processes to alter the structural characteristics of perovskites, which can impact the overall efficacy of the material.
  • Photoluminescence study of solution-deposited Cu2BaSnS4 thin films
    • Levcenko, S; Teymur, B; Mitzi, DB; Unold, T
    • The materials Cu2BaSnS4 (CBTS) and Cu2BaSnSe4 (CBTSe) have become a promising absorber for use in thin film solar cells. Due to their abundance and high band gap (ability for material to generate electricity), CBTS and CBTSe are quickly emerging as a commercial alternative to silicon.
  • David Mitzi: Harvesting More of the Sun’s Shine (Article)
    • Developing new materials that can compete with silicon and other emerging technologies in solar cells
    • This quick article provides a great introduction to Dr. Mitzi and his research goals. The article also quickly outlines the different silicon alternatives Dr. Mitzi is testing.

Energy Mapping

Energy Mapping

Summary

Kyle Bradbury, along with the Energy Data Analytics Lab, is utilizing machine learning techniques and satellite imaging to cultivate new data analytics in the energy sector. Through computer vision, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and the use of aerial imagery, the group has been able to develop to detect and map energy resources (such as solar panels, wind farms, etc).

The research lab hopes to one day create an automatically updating, global energy infrastructure map.

Publications
  • Electric Transmission Infrastructure Satellite Imagery Dataset for Computer Vision (2021)
    • Hu, W; Huang, B; Bradbury, K; Malof, J; Nair
    • This dataset contains fully annotated aerial imagery, marking electric infrastructure across 7 cities and 2 countries across 5 continents. This data was initially used as a training set to teach a machine learning algorithm to detect energy resources in satellite imagery.
  • GridTracer: Automatic Mapping of Power Grids using Deep Learning and Overhead Imagery
    • Bohao Huang; Jichen Yang; Artem Streltsov; Kyle Bradbury
    • Using deep learning models, this research group hopes to provide the world with a comprehensive, accessible means of mapping energy resources and infrastructure.
  • Estimating residential building energy consumption using overhead imagery
    • Artem Streltsov; Jordan M.Malof; Bohao Huang; Kyle Bradbury
    • This paper introduces a methodology of using satellite imagery and machine learning to automatically estimate indivudal building energy consumption. The research group hopes to use this data to better help reduce global energy consumption in residential buildings.
  • Do Deep Learning Models Generalize to Overhead Imagery from Novel Geographic Domains?
    • Bohao Huang; Kyle Bradbury; Leslie M. Collins; Jordan M. Malof
    • While convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have had impressive performance on accurately detecting features on satellite imagery, there has been concern that CNNs are only effective when trained and tested on imagery from similar geographical regions. This study aimed to determine the effectiveness of these machine learning models when given previously unseen aerial imagery from different geographical locations.

Hydrogen Fuel

Hydrogen Fuel

Summary

Nico Hotz is an Associate Professor of the Practice in the Department of Mechanical Engineering & Materials Science. With an overall focus in energy transfer, thermodynamics, and sustainable energy, Dr. Hotz has studied hydrogen gas as a highly efficient fuel alternative. Together, Dr. Hotz and his team have researched a wide variety of clean energy solutions, focusing specifically on the analysis of energy conversion and storage systems.

Publications
  • Renewable hydrogen production by solar-powered methanol reforming
    • Daniel Real, Ivonna Dumanyan, Nico Hotz
    • This study determined the feasibility in producing hydrogen through the process of methanol steam reforming. What's more, this procedure would take place inside a non-concentrating solar collector, which harnesses the sun's radiation to generate heat. If possible, this means of hydrogen production would be extremely sustainable (using the sun's energy to produce a non-polluting fuel), and ideal for stationary fuel cell systems.
  • NEW METHOD FOR PRODUCING CLEAN HYDROGEN (article)
    • Duke engineers devise new way to produce clean hydrogen
    • This article details a novel process of producing clean hydrogen. Current methodologies of creating hydrogen gas typically have carbon monoxide as a byproduct, which is dangerous humans, as well as the catalysts on fuel cell membranes. By switching out the nanoparticles used as catalyst for the hydrogen production reaction, this research team was able to reduce carbon monoxide byproduction to near zero.

Solar Fuel

Solar Fuel

Summary

Jeff Glass is a professor of Electrical and Computer Engineering, and currently runs a lab focused on developing electronic materials. His work ranges from creating a portable mass spectrometers (see CH4 Detection), to designing stretchable supercapacitators, and even inventing affordable toilets for developing nations.

In the realm of sustainability, Glass (along with the Nanomaterials and Thin Films Lab) has been researching the production of solar fuel, or the conversion from solar energy to chemical fuel using photoelectrochemical cells (PECs). Specifically, the group is assessing the feasibility in mass producing hydrogen gas, through concentrating solar energy enough to break water molecules. The lab, through developing new nanostructures to aid in this water splitting process, hopes to bring this technology into the energy industry as a sustainable alternative to fossil fuels.

Solar Materials

Summary

Volker Blum is the leader of the "Ab initio materials simulations" lab, which focues on "first-principles" computational materials science. To summarize, they use the fundamental laws of quantum mechanics to predict the properties of real materials, starting from the atomic scale.

Dr. Blum and his lab have used the computer packages and simulation tools available to them in order to investigate the feasiblitity in using various materials in photovoltaics.

Microbials

Summary

Michael Lynch is an Assistant Professor in the Biomedical Engineering Department. His lab specializes in the use of genetically engineered microbes to produce biofuels, pharmalogical molecules, and industrial chemicals.

The Lynch Lab hopes to use their unique approach to controlling microbial metabolism in order to produce biofuel, as a sustainable energy source.

Summary

Jeff Glass is a professor of Electrical and Computer Engineering, and currently runs a lab focused on developing electronic materials. His work ranges from creating a portable mass spectrometers, to designing stretchable supercapacitators, and even innovating toilets for developing nations.
